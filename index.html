01 — About

A practitioner first.
An operator by instinct.

I started my career on the floor — AR follow-up, denial work, insurance calling. Not in a strategy room. That distinction has shaped everything that came after.

Today I lead transitions for US Healthcare Revenue Cycle Management engagements at Infinx — owning the full lifecycle from sales handoff through hypercare stabilization. My work sits at the intersection of operations rigor, governance design, and AI-led process thinking.

What I've learned along the way: predictability is a creative output. Good transitions don't happen because someone wrote a Gantt chart — they happen because someone obsessed over the right details, built the right tools, and refused to ship until the system could run without them.

On weekends, sometimes, I write fiction. A short-story collection — Mostly Human, Occasionally Weird — was published by Notion Press in 2025. It's a quiet habit, not a second career. But it sharpens how I notice things at work the rest of the week.

05 — Leadership

How I lead.

Leadership in operations isn't about being the loudest voice in the room. It's about being the one person whose calm doesn't break when the go-live does.
01
Augment, don't replace Whether it's a new tool, a new framework, or AI — the test is whether it makes the team's work better, not whether it makes their work obsolete.
02
Predictability over heroics Last-minute saves get applause. But the team that doesn't need to save anything is the better-run team. I optimize for the second outcome.
03
Single point of accountability Every assigned transition has one owner. Distributed accountability is a polite phrase for nobody-is-responsible.
04
Honest with everyone Clients hear the real risk profile. Leadership sees the unvarnished health score. The team is told the truth about what's working and what isn't.
05
Build the tooling once If a problem shows up twice, the third time should be solved by a framework — not by someone's heroic late-night spreadsheet.
